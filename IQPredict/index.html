Few temporally distributed brain connectivity states predict human cognitive abilities

Abstract

Human functional brain connectivity can be temporally decomposed into states of high and low cofluctuation, defined as coactivation of brain regions over time. Rare states of particularly high cofluctuation have been shown to reflect fundamentals of intrinsic functional network architecture and to be highly subject-specific. However, it is unclear whether such network-defining states also contribute to individual variations in cognitive abilities, which strongly rely on the interactions among distributed brain regions. By introducing CMEP, a new eigenvector-based prediction framework, we show that as few as 16 temporally separated time frames (< 1.5% of 10min resting-state fMRI) can significantly predict individual differences in intelligence (N = 263, p < .001). Against previous expectations, individuals network-defining time frames of particularly high cofluctuation do not predict intelligence. Multiple functional brain networks contribute to the prediction, and all results replicate in an independent sample (N = 831). Our results suggest that although fundamentals of person-specific functional connectomes can be derived from few time frames of highest connectivity, temporally distributed information is necessary to extract information about cognitive abilities. This information is not restricted to specific connectivity states, like network-defining high-cofluctuation states, but rather reflected across the entire length of the brain connectivity time series.

Method - CMEP

We developed a two-staged machine learning-based predictive modelling framework, which we refer to as covariance maximizing eigenvector-based predictive modelling (CMEP). The basic idea of CMEP is to first create candidate features (based on eigenvectors) within a training set that share a strong linear relationship (covariance) with the target variable of interest, and then, in a second step, to assess the predictive power of these features by training and testing a prediction model (ElasticNet regression) based on these features.

Feature construction

Our new feature construction method explicitly amplifies features whose expression covary with a target variable (here intelligence). We project functional connectivity matrices into a scalar space such that the covariation between intelligence scores and this scalar projection is maximal.
